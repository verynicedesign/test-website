Context

A page built for marketing, not for people

Best Buy's sales events — Black Friday, Cyber Monday, Memorial Day, 4th of July — are high-pressure weeks where millions of customers arrive with urgency, purpose, and very little time. These moments drive a significant portion of annual revenue.

But the experience hadn't kept up. Deals were buried in long, unstructured grids. Teams rebuilt the same brittle pattern every year. There was no shared architecture, no personalization integration, and no foundation that could flex across events.

Research

Grounding in real customer behavior

Research spanned ContentSquare analytics, usability sessions, empathy mapping, and competitive benchmarking across Target, Amazon, Walmart, Etsy, and Sephora. Three distinct user archetypes emerged — each with different expectations for the same event page:

Seasonal Deal Hunter — arrived via paid search, price-first, skeptical. Needed bold event branding and instant deal access.

Engaged BBY Customer — returning shopper, logged in. Expected personalized deals relevant to past purchases.

Total Member — high-value, app-engaged. Motivated by exclusivity, member-first pricing, and early access.

The key insight: the same event page needed to serve three fundamentally different mental models — urgency-driven browsing, loyalty-driven relevance, and exclusivity-driven status — without fragmenting into three separate experiences.

System Design

Building a system, not a page

The old experience was a catalog. The new one needed to be a system — modular containers reusable across every event without rebuilding. We evaluated 7 AI layout approaches and selected a hybrid: defined top-down flow (Inspire → Discover → Convert) with AML powering product selection within each section.

Every module maps to a specific moment in the customer's mindset:

Validation

Techtober 2025 usability research

Before scaling system-wide, we ran usability sessions during Techtober — measuring comprehension, navigation, and engagement across real customer sessions.

What validated: Shoppers recognized event branding immediately and found products quickly. Red price treatment built trust. The category carousel made browsing intuitive. Several participants noticed personalization unprompted.

What we refined: Persistent event identity across filters and PLPs. Standardized deal signals (red + strike-through + event badge). "Back to Sale" anchor. Urgency cues moved above the fold.

Results

Measurable impact at massive scale

+14 basis points conversion lift — translating to $4M–$7M in annual revenue impact. +31 basis points mobile engagement lift — approximately $10M in influenced revenue. 10–15% increase in module interactions for priority SKUs and featured deal stories.

But the systemic impact is equally significant: the modular architecture now scales across 6+ annual events without the one-off rebuild cycle. Design-to-launch time dropped substantially. And the AML integration established a foundation for increasingly personalized experiences in future events.
